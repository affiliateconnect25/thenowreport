Gen Z Politics Are Different — Here’s Why

TikToks, Discords, and a deep mistrust in institutions — Gen Z is reshaping power on its own terms.

For a generation raised online, Gen Z approaches politics with a unique mix of skepticism, speed, and meme fluency. They aren’t tuning into C-SPAN — they’re watching TikToks between classes and organizing protests on Discord.

Politics Goes Mobile

Unlike previous generations, political awareness doesn’t begin with newspapers or TV anchors. It starts with infographics, threads, and viral clips. In a few scrolls, a 17-year-old can digest election analysis, foreign policy summaries, and protest plans.

This same speed, though, means misinformation spreads just as quickly. To survive it, Gen Z is developing media literacy on the fly — not as an academic exercise, but a form of online survival.

The Collapse of Trust

Their worldview is shaped by chaos: lockdowns, mass shootings, climate emergencies. Their trust in institutions is minimal. Instead of demanding change from the system, many are learning how to bypass it entirely.

A New Kind of Power

Movements are decentralized. Power is horizontal. Influencers, not party leaders, often lead discourse. Identity blends with political alignment in public and private life.

Older generations may scoff, but the reality is: Gen Z isn’t entering politics — they’re rebuilding it from the inside out.
